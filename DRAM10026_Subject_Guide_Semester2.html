Welcome to the Subject

This subject provides an inside look at the art, craft and culture of Melbourne Theatre Company (MTC). Emphasis is placed on you — the audience — and your appreciation of the work of the playwright, director, actor, designer, producer and critic. By the end of the semester you should be able to demonstrate a vocabulary of theatre terms and recognise the contributions of various theatre artists in an organisation like MTC.

You will attend two live MTC productions at Southbank Theatre — S. Shakthidharan and Eamon Flack's The Jungle and the Sea and Tom Holloway's Eliza — and develop your critical voice through curated video content, peer engagement and a culminating oral assessment. No theatre appreciation subject is complete without the live experience; the only prerequisite is your life experience and a desire to engage with performance.

Subject Information

Inside Look at MTC will enhance your knowledge of one of Melbourne's most significant cultural institutions and enliven the experience of going to the theatre in Australia's greatest city. You will be provided with access to two live productions at Southbank Theatre. Readings, videos and other materials are available online via Canvas.

These productions will be discussed throughout the semester; in doing so, we will consider how theatre contributes to the cultural conversations happening around us right now.

Learning Outcomes

On successful completion of this subject, you should be able to:

1
Use theatrical terms to analyse and evaluate methods of artistic and intellectual expression through theatre
2
Recognise and identify the contributions of key members of the collaborative team who create and perform theatre
3
Identify and explain basic social, cultural and political influences on theatre in an Australian context
4
Critically examine theatrical events using opinion, taste and cultural awareness
5
Identify basic elements of stagecraft and the techniques used for staging theatre

Assessment

There are three assessments for this subject. All assessments need to be attempted in order to qualify for a pass mark. Students will be assessed on their engagement with subject materials and their capacity to demonstrate the intended learning outcomes.

20%
MTC Quiz
Due Friday 21 August

An online quiz via Canvas asking you to identify key theatre concepts and terminology related to Melbourne Theatre Company, drawn from Modules 1–4.

50%
Interactive Oral Assessment
Week 12 · Wed 21 October · In Person

A comparative oral review of both MTC productions, delivered as a group discussion. In advance, you submit a 600-word written overview outlining your critical position on both shows. This is not a formal essay but a structured preparation document to anchor your contribution to the discussion.

In both the written overview and the oral discussion you should: discuss each production in terms of your own personal response and what you felt the makers were trying to achieve; demonstrate understanding of theatrical concepts and the roles of different creatives; draw comparisons between the two productions; and move beyond simple opinion into why certain choices were made and what the experience of watching each production was actually like.

The discussion is a genuine conversation, not a presentation. Listening and responding to your peers is part of what is being assessed.

Academic tone and referencing conventions are not appropriate — this is your response as an informed audience member. Examples of theatre reviews and critical discussions are provided across Modules 6 to 8. An assessment rubric is available on the subject page.
30%
Research Journal
Full journal due Friday 6 November · Drafts at specified points

Throughout the semester, the videos you watch in each module contain embedded provocations: questions and prompts designed to spark reflection and inquiry. These provocations are your Research Journal entry points.

You will develop four journal entries in response to these provocations, drawing on a combination of curated videos and your own web-based research. Drafts are submitted at two checkpoints during the semester — Checkpoint 1 (Entries 1 and 2) on Friday 28 August, and Checkpoint 2 (Entries 3 and 4) on Friday 9 October — each followed by a round of peer feedback through FeedbackFruits, with your complete polished journal due during the assessment period.

Your journal can take many forms. Written entries are welcome, but so are creative responses: video essays, sketches, photographs, performance documentation, or any other form that engages seriously with the material.

Creative and theatrical approaches that demonstrate genuine intellectual engagement will be recognised in your mark. Your final mark reflects the quality of your entries and your sustained engagement with the draft-and-feedback cycle with your peers throughout the semester.

Use of Generative AI

You are not permitted to use an AI tool in any way when completing your work for this subject. Any use suspected will be reported as potential academic misconduct and subject to appropriate penalties.

This restriction applies to all three assessments — the MTC Quiz, the Research Journal, and the Interactive Oral Assessment, including the 600-word written overview submitted before your IOA session.

It also applies at every stage of preparing your work, not only to final submitted text. This includes brainstorming or discussing ideas with an AI assistant, generating outlines or plans, drafting or rewriting passages, generating images, video or other content, and using AI tools for proofreading, editing, paraphrasing or translation.

For clarity, tools covered by this restriction include (but are not limited to) ChatGPT, Claude, Microsoft Copilot, Gemini, Grammarly, QuillBot, and image generators such as Midjourney, DALL·E or Stable Diffusion.

Why this subject takes this position

The work of this subject is your own response as an audience member. The Research Journal asks what you noticed, felt and thought in a theatre; the IOA asks you to hold and defend that view in conversation with your peers. There is no version of that work a machine can do for you — and an entry or overview you did not write yourself gives your peers nothing to respond to in the feedback cycle, and gives you nothing to say in the room. The point is not to produce a text; it is to develop your own critical voice.

Your writing does not need to be polished or academic. We are far more interested in an honest, uncertain, first-person response than in fluent prose. Please do not reach for a tool to make your writing sound more impressive — that is not what is being assessed.

Declaration

You will be asked to include a short declaration at the end of the Research Journal and the IOA Overview confirming that no AI tools were used:

Declaration
I have not used any AI tools or technologies to prepare this assessment.

If you are unsure

Asking is never an admission of guilt. If you are uncertain whether something counts — a spellchecker, a translation tool, a browser feature you did not choose to switch on — ask Goran or Chris before you use it, not after you submit. We would far rather have that conversation early.

The University uses Turnitin's AI writing detection on submitted work. Where unacknowledged use is suspected, the matter is handled under the Student Academic Integrity Policy (MPF1310) and penalties follow the Schedule of Student Academic Misconduct Penalties.

Further University guidance for students is available at Acknowledging AI tools and technologies.

University Policies

The following university policies apply to all students enrolled in this subject. Click each section to expand.

Extensions

Students may be granted extensions of up to two weeks (10 working days) to accommodate unforeseen extenuating circumstances, where a student's capacity to complete required work by the due date has been affected by significant factors beyond their control.

Students requesting extensions longer than two weeks should apply for Special Consideration instead.

Applications for extensions must be submitted before the due date of the assignment, unless exceptional circumstances prevent a timely application. Submission of an application does not mean automatic approval. Students should continue to complete work by the due date until advised of the outcome.

Eligible circumstances include: illness or medical condition (with certificate), injury or trauma, death or serious illness of a close family member, illness of a dependent for whom the student is primary caregiver, unforeseen events substantially impacting preparation, and legal commitments such as jury duty.

Extensions will not be granted for: computer failure, assessment tasks in other subjects, employment responsibilities, social activities, normal stress or anxiety, study difficulties, language difficulties, minor inconvenience, or performance-related commitments without Elite Performer registration.

If you're unsure about supporting documentation, contact fineartsmusicspecialcon@unimelb.edu.au.

Late Submission

Where no extension or special consideration has been sought, late submissions will receive the following penalties:

10% of the maximum mark per working day (or part thereof) the assessment is overdue, where work is less than a week overdue. A pass mark capped at 50% where work is more than a week overdue. Assignments submitted two weeks after the deadline will not be marked and receive a mark of zero.

Academic Integrity

The Faculty of Fine Arts and Music expects all students to conduct themselves in a manner which is fair, honest and consistent with the principles of academic integrity.

Academic work submitted for assessment must be the original work of the author. If the ideas or words of others have been drawn upon, this must be thoroughly and clearly acknowledged using agreed scholarly conventions.

For full details, see the Student Academic Integrity Policy (MPF1310) and the Academic Integrity Module (AIM) on Canvas. Additional information is available at the University Academic Integrity website.

Plagiarism, collusion and all other forms of academic misconduct are serious offences managed according to the Student Academic Integrity Policy.
